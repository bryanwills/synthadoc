# Personal Computer Revolution

The personal computer revolution of the 1970s and 1980s brought computing from air-conditioned mainframe rooms into homes and small businesses, fundamentally democratising access to programmable machines.

## The Altair 8800 (1975)

MITS's Altair 8800, sold as a kit for $439, is the symbolic starting point of the PC era. Based on Intel's 8080 processor, it had no keyboard and no display — users toggled binary switches on the front panel. Despite this, it sold thousands of units and triggered a hobbyist ecosystem. Critically, it attracted two Harvard students — Bill Gates and Paul Allen — who wrote a BASIC interpreter for it and founded Microsoft.

## Apple II (1977)

Steve Wozniak designed the Apple II around the MOS 6502 processor. It was the first mass-market computer with a colour display, an open expansion bus, and a plastic case designed for a home environment. VisiCalc (1979), the first spreadsheet, ran on the Apple II and became the first "killer app" — people bought the computer to run the software, not the other way around.

## IBM PC and the Open Architecture (1981)

IBM's decision to build the PC from off-the-shelf parts and publish the technical specifications was the most consequential business decision of the era. It licensed the operating system from Microsoft (MS-DOS) and the CPU from Intel. Within two years, dozens of manufacturers cloned the platform legally, creating a commodity hardware market. The architecture — x86 CPU, BIOS, DOS — became the default standard and still underlies most Windows machines today.

## Graphical User Interface

Xerox PARC developed the first practical GUI with the Alto (1973), but it was Apple's Macintosh (1984) that brought windows, icons, and a mouse to a mainstream audience. Microsoft followed with Windows 1.0 (1985). The GUI made [[programming-languages-overview]] tools accessible to users who would never write code.

## Relationship to Other Pages

The personal computer runs on [[von-neumann-architecture]] hardware. Its operating systems descend from [[unix-history]] (macOS, Linux desktop) or from the DOS/Windows lineage. The [[transistor-and-microchip]] revolution made the microprocessors that power PCs possible. The [[internet-origins]] web gave PCs their "always-on" purpose in the 1990s.

## Technological Foundations

The personal computer revolution rested on three successive breakthroughs that made programmable computing physically and economically feasible outside the mainframe room: the [[transistor-and-microchip|transistor]] (1947), the integrated circuit (1959), and the microprocessor (1971).

### The Transistor (1947)

On 16 December 1947, John Bardeen, Walter Brattain, and William Shockley at Bell Labs demonstrated the first point-contact transistor. Replacing fragile, heat-generating vacuum tubes, transistors were small, reliable, and power-efficient — prerequisites for any machine intended to sit on a desk.

### The Integrated Circuit (1959)

In 1959, two engineers independently solved the problem of interconnecting many transistors on a single piece of semiconductor. Jack Kilby of Texas Instruments demonstrated the germanium IC, while Robert Noyce of Fairchild Semiconductor built the first silicon IC using a planar process. The integrated circuit collapsed entire circuits onto a single chip, enabling further miniaturisation and a dramatic drop in cost per logic gate.

### The Microprocessor (1971)

The microprocessor — a full CPU on one IC — arrived in 1971 with Intel's 4004, a 4-bit chip designed for a Japanese calculator. By putting the arithmetic and control logic of a computer onto a single sliver of silicon, the microprocessor made it economically possible to build programmable machines for homes, schools, and small businesses rather than only for governments and large corporations.

### Moore's Law

Underpinning all three transitions was the empirical trend articulated by Gordon Moore in 1965: roughly doubling of transistor density every two years, accompanied by falling cost per transistor. This [[moore-s-law|Moore's Law]] trajectory gave the industry a roadmap and a self-fulfilling cadence for miniaturisation, which in turn fed directly into the falling prices that made machines like the Altair 8800 possible just four years after the first microprocessor.

## Technological Foundations

The personal computer revolution rested on roughly two decades of hardware progress that shrunk room-sized mainframes into desktop-sized machines.

### From Vacuum Tubes to Transistors

Early electronic computers such as [[eniac]] used thousands of vacuum tubes, which were bulky, power-hungry, and prone to failure. On 16 December 1947, john-bardeen, walter-brattain, and william-shockley at bell-labs demonstrated the first point-contact transistor, a semiconductor device that could amplify and switch signals while consuming far less power. The transistor rapidly displaced vacuum tubes in computer design.

### The Integrated Circuit (1959)

The next leap in miniaturisation came with the integrated circuit (IC), independently invented in 1959 by jack-kilby at texas-instruments and robert-noyce at fairchild-semiconductor. By fabricating multiple transistors on a single piece of semiconductor material, the IC made it possible to build complex circuits reliably and at scale.

### Moore's Law (1965)

In 1965, gordon-moore, then at Fairchild Semiconductor, observed that the number of components per integrated circuit was doubling roughly every year (later revised to every two years). This trend, known as [[moore-s-law]], became the semiconductor industry's self-fulfilling roadmap for decades.

### The Intel 4004 (1971)

The first commercially available single-chip microprocessor was the intel-4004, released by Intel in November 1971. Designed by ted-hoff and stanley-mazor under the leadership of federico-faggin, the 4004 was originally commissioned by the Japanese calculator company busicom. It contained 2,300 transistors on a 12 mm² die and was a 4-bit CPU, demonstrating that an entire processor could fit on one IC.

### The Path to the Desktop

Transistor-based mainframes such as the ibm-7090 (introduced in 1959) proved that solid-state electronics could outperform vacuum-tube designs in both speed and reliability. Subsequent microprocessor generations — including the 8-bit intel-8080 that powered the altair-8800 and the 16-bit intel-8086 that defined the x86 architecture — carried this scaling directly into homes and small businesses.

## VisiCalc and the First Killer App (1979)

VisiCalc, the first electronic spreadsheet, was written by [[john-bardeen|Dan Bricklin]] and Bob Frankston for the [[apple-ii|Apple II]] and released in 1979. VisiCalc allowed users to perform financial calculations interactively, updating a grid of values automatically when any cell changed. It became the first example of what would be called a **killer application** — software so useful that people purchased hardware specifically to run it. VisiCalc drove Apple II sales and established the personal computer as a legitimate business tool, not merely a hobbyist curiosity. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:33-33]

## IBM PC and the Open Architecture (1981)

IBM entered the personal computer market in August 1981 with the IBM Personal Computer. Rather than developing proprietary technology, IBM built the PC from commercially available components — the Intel 8088 processor, memory chips, and peripherals from multiple suppliers — and published the full technical specifications. This open architecture allowed other manufacturers to build IBM-compatible machines legally. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:37-37]

IBM licensed its operating system from [[microsoft]], which had acquired it (as QDOS) from Seattle Computer Products and adapted it as MS-DOS. The decision to license rather than own the operating system became one of the most consequential business errors in computing history: as dozens of clone manufacturers entered the market, IBM rapidly lost its ability to control the platform. The x86 architecture, running DOS and later Windows, became the enduring industry standard. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:39-39]

## Macintosh and the Graphical User Interface (1984)

Xerox PARC (Palo Alto Research Center) developed the first practical graphical user interface in the early 1970s on the Alto workstation. The Alto introduced overlapping windows, icons, a mouse-driven pointer, and pull-down menus. Although Xerox did not successfully commercialise the Alto, Apple engineers — including Steve Jobs — who visited PARC in 1979 absorbed the approach and carried it into commercial products. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:43-43]

Apple's Lisa (1983) and Macintosh (1984) brought the graphical user interface to mass-market computing. The Macintosh, priced at $2,495, combined a GUI with a mouse and a built-in display in a compact, integrated consumer design. It established the visual, direct-manipulation paradigm — application icons, windows, menus, mouse pointer — that [[microsoft]] followed with Windows 1.0 in 1985 and that became the standard model of personal computing for decades. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:45-45]

## First-Generation Context: ENIAC and Vacuum Tubes

To appreciate the scale of the PC revolution, contrast the Altair with the machines it replaced conceptually. ENIAC, the most famous vacuum-tube computer of the late 1940s, occupied 1,800 square feet, contained 17,468 vacuum tubes, and required constant maintenance as tubes burned out. The transistor, demonstrated at Bell Labs in December 1947, made possible the multi-decade miniaturisation — from room-sized mainframes to single-chip microprocessors like the [[intel-4004|Intel 4004]] (1971, 2,300 transistors) — that eventually put a CPU on every hobbyist's workbench. ^[ceruzzi-history-modern-computing.txt:7-7]
